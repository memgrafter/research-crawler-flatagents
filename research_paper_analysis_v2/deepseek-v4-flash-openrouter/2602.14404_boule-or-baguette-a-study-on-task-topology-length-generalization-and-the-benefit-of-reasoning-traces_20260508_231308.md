# Boule or Baguette? A Study on Task Topology, Length Generalization, and the Benefit of Reasoning Traces

## Quick Facts

- **arXiv ID:** 2602.14404
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14404
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** Introducing the PITA dataset (23M propositional logic statements with proofs), the paper shows that reasoning trace (RT) models generalize well on broad, shallow tasks (boule splits) but deteriorate on narrow, deep tasks (baguette splits), where direct prediction (DP) baselines outperform RT by up to 50 percentage points.

## Executive Summary

This paper investigates length generalization in reasoning trace (RT) models compared to direct prediction (DP) models. The authors introduce PITA, a large dataset of propositional logic statements with Lean proofs, and define task topology in terms of depth (proof steps) and breadth (unique examples). Across three LLM families (Qwen2.5-Coder, Gemma 3, Llama 3), RT models excel on broad, shallow tasks but fail on narrow, deep tasks relative to DP baselines.

A theoretical analysis on transitive inference reveals fundamental scaling limitations: RT generalization decays as depth scales with the square root of model width (D ∝ O(√H)), while DP scales linearly (D ∝ O(H)). The paper attributes RT strengths to permutation invariance enabling generalization on shallow tasks and DP strengths to avoiding long-context statistical dilution on deep tasks. The work raises important questions about when and why reasoning traces are beneficial, challenging the assumption that chain-of-thought reasoning universally improves generalization.

## Method Summary

The study uses two experimental setups. First, the authors create the PITA dataset containing 23M propositional logic statements with proofs in the Lean theorem prover. They define four splits (FULL, IMPLY, OR, PHP) with controlled depth and breadth. Pretrained LLMs (Qwen2.5-Coder, Gemma 3, Llama 3) are finetuned via QLoRA (rank 16, α=32, dropout 0.05) on these splits. RT models receive the full proof chain as intermediate tokens; DP models output the conclusion directly. Generalization accuracy is measured on test examples requiring longer proofs than seen during training.

Second, a synthetic transitive inference (TI) task is used for controlled theoretical analysis. The authors train single-layer, single-head decoder-only Transformers from scratch (no positional encodings) on symbol pairs with transitive relations. RT models receive full symbol enumerations; DP models output binary classification directly. Training uses AdamW with learning rate 1e-2, 100k iterations, and varying model widths H (64, 128, 256). This setup allows precise measurement of how depth (D symbols per branch) and breadth (B branches) affect generalization.

## Key Results

- On the PITA dataset, RT models outperform DP on broad, shallow (boule) splits but DP outperforms RT by up to 50 percentage points on narrow, deep (baguette) splits across all three LLM families.
- In transitive inference, RT generalization accuracy decays as depth increases, with the maximum generalizable depth scaling as D ∝ O(√H), while DP scales as D ∝ O(H).
- DP models show accuracy decay proportional to O(B⁻²) as breadth increases due to overfitting to training distribution heuristics, while RT models maintain high accuracy with increasing breadth.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1

- **Claim:** Reasoning trace (RT) models generalize well on broad, shallow tasks because the trace provides an inductive bias that makes train and test examples equivalent under permutation invariance.
- **Mechanism:** A reasoning trace enumerates intermediate steps so that for any test example (with longer proof steps), there exists a permutation of the trace that exactly matches a training example. Without positional encodings, the model treats that permuted input as a training example, giving perfect generalization.
- **Core assumption:** The model uses no positional encodings, so it is invariant to token order.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract] "RT models generalize well on broad and shallow subsets."
  - [section] Appendix B, Proposition B.4: "For any test example, we may construct a permutation π such that ... is a training example."
  - [corpus] Weak. No neighbor paper directly tests permutation invariance for generalization on shallow tasks.
- **Break condition:** Positional encodings are added, or the enumeration scheme does not cover all intermediate symbols (e.g., early termination).

### Mechanism 2

- **Claim:** Direct prediction (DP) models outperform RT models on deep, narrow tasks because DP avoids long-context statistical dilution, while RT's accuracy is bounded by a square-root dependence on context length.
- **Mechanism:** In transitive inference, the RT model's input grows linearly with depth. The distributions of positive and negative examples become harder to distinguish, with total variation distance decaying as O(√H/D). This limits RT accuracy. DP models process only two tokens, avoiding this dilution.
- **Core assumption:** Symbol embeddings are fixed Gaussian, so the sum over many tokens blends signal.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract] "DP outperforming RT by up to 50 percentage points on deep splits."
  - [section] Proposition B.3: "TV(F, T) = O(√H/L)... accuracy is also bounded by 1/2 + O(√H/D)."
  - [corpus] Weak. No neighbor paper studies statistical dilution from long context in reasoning traces.
- **Break condition:** The model uses a mechanism to suppress noise from irrelevant context tokens (e.g., sparse attention) or the task has no irrelevant context.

### Mechanism 3

- **Claim:** RT models maintain high generalization accuracy as breadth increases, while DP models' accuracy decays as O(B⁻²) due to overfitting to training distribution heuristics.
- **Mechanism:** DP models learn a "short-cut" solution where negative examples accumulate O(B) contributions that push all logits negative. This causes false negatives on test examples. RT models, by explicitly enumerating intermediates, achieve a solution that generalizes without this accumulator.
- **Core assumption:** Negative examples are formed from different branches, and each branch contributes an independent negative signal to the logit.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract] "Our resulting theory suggests fundamental scalings that limit how well RT models perform on deep tasks, and highlights their generalization strengths on broad tasks."
  - [section] Section B.5: "Pr(ŷ > 0) = O(B⁻²)... tradition accuracy falls with increasing task breadth."
  - [corpus] Weak. No neighbor paper studies breadth-scaling of generalization in DP vs RT models.
- **Break condition:** The training distribution includes both same-branch and different-branch negative examples in a balanced way, or the DP model is regularized to avoid accumulating contributions.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:** Input → Embeddings (frozen Gaussians) → Single-head Self-Attention (no position encoding) → MLP with ReLU activation → Linear readout → Binary logit.

- **Critical path:** The MLP output depends on the sum of token activations (uniform attention). For RT models, the sum includes all enumeration tokens. The sign of the logit is determined by which branches have positive/negative sample coordinates.

- **Design tradeoffs:** Deeper tasks require longer RT context, which dilutes signal (TV decay). Increasing model width H improves both depth scaling (H vs √H) and breadth scaling (H²) but at different rates. Using positional encodings might fix permutation invariance but break the generalization guarantee from Proposition B.4.

- **Failure signatures:** RT model fails to terminate within max context (shown in Figure 10 as "None" category). DP model shows high false negative rate on deeper test examples. The model output is syntactically correct Lean code but uses wrong tactics (e.g., apply False.elim when no False atom exists).

- **First 3 experiments:**
  1. Train a single-layer, single-head Transformer on the transitive inference task with varying depth (10, 20, 30) and fixed breadth (50), measuring test accuracy for DP vs RT models. Replicate the TV bound: does accuracy drop as 1/2 + O(1/√depth)?
  2. Train on the PITA IMPLY split with RT model, then remove the <backtrack> tags from training data. Measure whether performance on deep test examples changes, testing if backtrack tokens are critical for long traces.
  3. Train a DP model on the PITA OR split and measure the false negative rate on test examples. Is the rate proportional to B⁻², or does it saturate? This tests the generality of the scaling result from transitive inference.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1

- **Question:** Can the theoretical scaling laws (D ∝ H for DP, D ∝ √H for RT) derived for transitive inference be empirically verified on the more complex PITA dataset?
- **Basis in paper:** [explicit] "Our resulting theory suggests fundamental scalings..." (Section 4) and the TI analysis in Appendix B.
- **Why unresolved:** The paper proposes these scalings for TI but notes PITA "is substantially simpler than PITA, and different mechanisms may drive the topology/RT relationships."
- **What evidence would resolve it:** Controlled experiments on PITA sub-splits varying model width and measuring the depth at which accuracy drops below a threshold.

### Open Question 2

- **Question:** Does the DP model's superior performance on baguette-shaped splits persist when the reasoning traces are truncated or when long-context processing is improved?
- **Basis in paper:** [inferred] The authors attribute RT failure on deep tasks to long-context issues (Section 4) and show "failure to terminate accounts for a large proportion of RT errors" (Figure 10).
- **Why unresolved:** The paper does not test interventions like sliding window attention or context compression.
- **What evidence would resolve it:** Comparing RT performance with and without long-context mitigations (e.g., sparse attention, position interpolation) on PHP splits.

### Open Question 3

- **Question:** How does the RT vs. DP performance trade-off generalize to other reasoning domains beyond propositional logic and transitive inference?
- **Basis in paper:** [explicit] "Our notions of task depth and breadth are general, and we anticipate future work characterizing topology more precisely and across a wider range of settings" (Section 4).
- **Why unresolved:** The paper only tests two domains (PITA and synthetic TI), leaving the universality of the boule/baguette distinction unverified.
- **What evidence would resolve it:** Construct datasets with controlled depth/breadth in domains like arithmetic, planning, or code generation, then replicate the generalization experiment.

## Limitations

- Mechanism 1 assumes no positional encodings, but all tested LLMs (Qwen2.5, Gemma, Llama) use positional encodings, making the core theoretical assumption invalid for the main experiments.
- Mechanism 2 relies on fixed Gaussian embeddings and uniform attention, which has not been empirically verified in trained transformers.
- Mechanism 3's breadth scaling (O(B⁻²)) is derived only for the synthetic TI task and not validated on the PITA dataset.
- The PITA proof pipeline (focused search parameters, Lean version, backtrack generation specifics) is underspecified, hindering faithful reproduction.

## Confidence

- **RT generalization on shallow/broad tasks (Mechanism 1): Low** — the critical assumption does not hold in the main experiments.
- **RT depth scaling limitation (Mechanism 2): Medium** — theoretical bound is plausible but only confirmed on a toy setting; transfer to LLMs is speculative.
- **DP breadth scaling disadvantage (Mechanism 3): Medium** — supported by TI results but unverified on real propositional logic data.
