# Boule or Baguette? A Study on Task Topology, Length Generalization, and the Benefit of Reasoning Traces

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2602.14404
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14404
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** Reasoning traces help on broad, shallow tasks but can hurt on narrow, deep tasks; RT beats DP on “boule-shaped” splits and lags badly on “baguette-shaped” splits.

## Executive Summary
This paper studies when reasoning traces (RT) improve or degrade length generalization in neural reasoning. Using PITA, a Lean-formalized propositional logic dataset at very large scale, the authors compare RT models against direct-prediction (DP) baselines under a training regime that stops at the median proof length and tests on longer proofs.

Their main finding is a topology-dependent reversal: RT models perform better on broad, shallow tasks and worse on narrow, deep ones. The authors formalize this with task depth and breadth, and show the effect across four splits—FULL, IMPLY, OR, and PHP—plus a transitive-inference toy task that exhibits the same qualitative trend.

## Method Summary
The study constructs PITA, a large Lean-formalized propositional-logic benchmark, and partitions it into four task splits designed to vary in breadth and depth: FULL, IMPLY, OR, and PHP. Models are trained on proofs up to the median proof length and evaluated on longer proofs to probe length generalization rather than in-distribution accuracy.

The core comparison is between reasoning-trace models and direct-prediction baselines. The paper then interprets the resulting performance patterns through the lens of task topology, using breadth and depth as descriptive variables for why some tasks benefit from intermediate reasoning traces while others do not.

## Key Results
- RT models outperform DP on broad, shallow “boule-shaped” splits (FULL, IMPLY).
- RT models underperform DP on narrow, deep “baguette-shaped” splits (OR, PHP), with gaps as large as ~50 percentage points on PHP for the 7B model.
- A transitive-inference toy task reproduces the same reversal pattern; DP degrades with breadth roughly like B^-2, while RT stays flatter on breadth but scales worse with depth.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The paper’s central explanation is geometric/topological rather than purely algorithmic: the benefit of reasoning traces depends on whether the task distribution is broad or deep. In broad, shallow settings, intermediate traces appear to regularize or scaffold generalization; in narrow, deep settings, they may add unnecessary steps, compounding errors and making longer proofs harder to extrapolate.

A useful way to read the result is that RT changes the effective path the model must traverse at test time. When the task space is wide but not too deep, that path can help organize search and compositional reuse. When the task space is narrow but proof chains are long, direct prediction may avoid the overhead and fragility introduced by explicit traces.

## Foundational Learning
- **Task depth vs. breadth**
  - **Why needed:** These are the paper’s main explanatory variables for why RT helps or hurts.
  - **Quick check:** Can you distinguish “many short variants” from “few long chains” in a benchmark?

- **Length generalization**
  - **Why needed:** The evaluation target is performance on proofs longer than those seen during training.
  - **Quick check:** What happens when train-time proof lengths are truncated at a median threshold?

- **Reasoning traces vs. direct prediction**
  - **Why needed:** The paper’s comparison isolates whether emitting intermediate steps helps generalize.
  - **Quick check:** Does the model predict the answer directly, or must it generate a proof-like trace first?

- **Topological task shape (“boule” vs. “baguette”)**
  - **Why needed:** This is the shorthand for the reversal phenomenon.
  - **Quick check:** Is the task broad/shallow or narrow/deep?

- **Proof-length scaling behavior**
  - **Why needed:** The toy task suggests different scaling laws for RT and DP under increasing breadth/depth.
  - **Quick check:** Does accuracy decay primarily with breadth, depth, or both?

## Architecture Onboarding
Component map: PITA / toy task -> train-on-median-length split -> RT model vs. DP baseline -> longer-proof test set -> breadth/depth analysis -> boule/baguette conclusion

Critical path: dataset topology design -> controlled length-generalization protocol -> model comparison -> split-wise performance reversal -> scaling interpretation

Design tradeoffs:
- RT provides explicit intermediate structure, which can help on broad tasks but adds overhead on deep ones.
- DP is simpler and may be more robust when long chains make trace generation brittle.
- The breadth/depth framing is interpretable, but it may compress other influences such as symbol frequency, class imbalance, and proof-search difficulty.

Failure signatures:
- RT underperforming DP on narrow/deep splits, especially PHP.
- Performance collapsing as proof length exceeds the training cutoff.
- Apparent topology effects that might actually come from leakage, duplicates, or distribution shifts.

Exactly 3 first experiments:
1. Train RT and DP with multiple seeds on each split and compare the RT-vs-DP gap with confidence intervals.
2. Vary the proof-length cutoff and check whether the boule/baguette reversal persists across budgets.
3. Repeat the comparison across model sizes or architectures to see whether the topology effect is model-specific.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
- How much of the reversal is truly explained by breadth/depth, versus symbol frequency, proof-search structure, or class imbalance?
- Do RT and DP behave differently under other decoding strategies or training budgets?
- Is the reported topology effect stable across more model families and architectures?

## Limitations
- The strongest claim is the relative performance reversal; its magnitude likely depends on model family, training recipe, decoding setup, and proof-length cutoff.
- PITA’s scale is large, but the dataset composition, split construction, and leakage controls are not independently verified here.
- The breadth/depth framing is descriptive and may not be the only driver of generalization behavior.

## Confidence
- **High:** RT helps on broad/shallow tasks and hurts on narrow/deep tasks.
- **Medium:** The toy transitive-inference result and the stated scaling trends.
- **Medium-Low:** The exact quantitative effect sizes, including the ~50-point PHP gap for the 7B model.

## Next Checks
1. Re-run the main split comparisons with multiple seeds and report confidence intervals for the RT-vs-DP gap.
2. Audit PITA split construction for leakage, near-duplicates, and topology-induced shifts unrelated to depth/breadth.
3. Test whether the reversal persists under alternative decoding/training budgets and across more model sizes or architectures.
