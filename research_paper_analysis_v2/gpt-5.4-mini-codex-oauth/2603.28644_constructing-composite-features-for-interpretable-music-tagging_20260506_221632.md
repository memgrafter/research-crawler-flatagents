# Constructing Composite Features for Interpretable Music-Tagging

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2603.28644
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.28644
- **Reference count:** 0
- **Primary result:** A GP-generated composite-feature pipeline slightly improved MTG-Jamendo ROC-AUC with XGBoost and more noticeably improved GTZAN accuracy, while keeping evolved expressions human-readable.

## Executive Summary
This paper targets a common tradeoff in music tagging: feature fusion can improve performance, but the resulting combinations are often opaque. The authors propose a Genetic Programming (GP) pipeline that evolves composite features by mathematically combining base audio features into readable expressions, then appends those features to an XGBoost tagger.

The reported gains are modest on MTG-Jamendo and larger on GTZAN, suggesting the approach can help, but also that benchmark choice and evaluation details likely matter a lot. The main practical contribution is not just accuracy uplift, but a procedure for generating interpretable composite features with explicit expression forms and controlled complexity.

## Method Summary
The core pipeline uses Genetic Programming to search over compositions of base audio features, producing candidate expressions that are intended to remain human-readable. These evolved composite features are added to a downstream XGBoost model for music tagging, so the final system is still a standard supervised learner, but with an augmented feature set.

The summary indicates the search favors compact expressions via parsimony pressure, and the best solutions often emerge early in the GP run. The reported comparison includes both ALL62 and E23 feature sets, showing that GP augmentation can improve performance under different feature budgets.

## Key Results
- On **MTG-Jamendo**, ROC-AUC improved from **0.727 to 0.730** with **ALL62** and from **0.719 to 0.724** with **E23**.
- On **GTZAN**, accuracy improved from **0.765 to 0.805** with **ALL62** and from **0.740 to 0.790** with **E23**.
- Most gains appeared within the first few hundred GP evaluations; best expressions included linear, nonlinear, and conditional forms.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The mechanism is feature discovery under an interpretability constraint. Instead of relying only on raw or hand-selected descriptors, GP searches over mathematical combinations of existing audio features to create higher-level signals that may better align with tag labels.

The likely benefit comes from two sources: richer feature interactions and controlled complexity. By imposing parsimony pressure, the search can keep expressions short enough to inspect, which helps avoid the usual “black-box fusion” problem while still allowing nonlinear or conditional structure when it is useful.

## Foundational Learning
- **Genetic Programming**
  - Why needed: it automates the search for useful feature formulas rather than requiring manual engineering.
  - Quick check: can it evolve compact expressions without overfitting to the training split?

- **Composite feature construction**
  - Why needed: music tags often depend on interactions among low-level descriptors, not single features alone.
  - Quick check: do the learned combinations outperform simple one-feature baselines or handcrafted interactions?

- **Parsimony pressure**
  - Why needed: keeps evolved formulas small enough to interpret and less likely to memorize noise.
  - Quick check: does tighter complexity control preserve most of the gain while making expressions simpler?

- **XGBoost as downstream tagger**
  - Why needed: provides a strong, practical classifier that can exploit new features without changing the whole modeling stack.
  - Quick check: do GP features still help when plugged into a different classifier?

- **Music-tagging evaluation**
  - Why needed: performance can vary widely across datasets, metrics, and split protocols.
  - Quick check: are improvements stable across datasets and repeated runs?

## Architecture Onboarding
Component map: base audio features -> GP search over feature expressions -> selected composite features -> XGBoost tagger -> music-tag predictions

Critical path: define base features and fitness objective -> evolve candidate expressions -> filter by parsimony/quality -> augment feature set -> train XGBoost -> evaluate on benchmark splits

Design tradeoffs:
- Interpretability vs. expressive power: shorter formulas are easier to read but may miss useful complexity.
- Search cost vs. gains: GP adds optimization overhead for sometimes small performance improvements.
- Generality vs. benchmark fit: expressions may work well on one dataset but not transfer cleanly elsewhere.

Failure signatures:
- Little or no gain over the baseline after GP augmentation.
- Very complex expressions that are hard to interpret despite parsimony pressure.
- Performance that changes sharply across seeds, splits, or datasets.
- Gains concentrated only on a small benchmark, suggesting overfitting or split sensitivity.

## Limitations
- MTG-Jamendo gains are small, and GTZAN results may be sensitive to split/preprocessing choices.
- It is unclear how much improvement comes from GP feature construction versus added capacity or benchmark-specific effects.
- The summary does not report significance, variance across seeds, or transfer across tags/datasets.

## Confidence
- GP can produce readable composite features -> High
- Small MTG-Jamendo gains over XGBoost baseline -> High
- GTZAN improvement magnitude/generalizability -> Medium
- Broad claim of better interpretable music tagging -> Low
