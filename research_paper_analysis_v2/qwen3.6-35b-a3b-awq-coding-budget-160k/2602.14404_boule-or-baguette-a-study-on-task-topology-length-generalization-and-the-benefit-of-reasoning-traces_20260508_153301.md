# Boule or Baguette? A Study on Task Topology, Length Generalization, and the Benefit of Reasoning Traces

## Quick Facts
*   **arXiv ID:** 2602.14404
*   **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14404
*   **Reference Count:** 40
*   **Primary Result:** Reasoning traces outperform direct prediction in shallow/broad ("boule") task topologies, while direct prediction surpasses reasoning traces by up to 50 percentage points in deep/narrow ("baguette") topologies.

## Executive Summary
Tong, Cakar, and Pehlevan investigate the limits of reasoning traces (RTs) in length generalization using PITA, a synthetic dataset of 23 million propositional logic statements. By defining task topology through proof depth and example breadth, the study categorizes generalization scenarios into "boule" (shallow/broad) and "baguette" (deep/narrow) shapes. The research evaluates Qwen2.5-Coder, Gemma 3, and Llama 3 models to determine how task structure influences the efficacy of step-by-step reasoning versus direct prediction.

The core finding challenges the assumption that reasoning traces are universally superior for complex logic. While RT models maintain high accuracy as task breadth increases, they suffer significant performance drops in deep tasks due to statistical difficulties in processing long contexts. Conversely, direct prediction models exhibit robustness in narrow, deep settings, suggesting that architectural inductive biases and signal dispersion play critical roles in generalization limits.

## Method Summary
The study utilizes the PITA dataset, comprising 23 million propositional logic statements, to isolate the effects of task topology on model performance. Tasks are categorized by two variables: depth (the number of proof steps required) and breadth (the number of unique examples). This creates a taxonomy of tasks ranging from simple, frequently seen examples to complex, novel logical chains.

Models are trained and evaluated on splits of this dataset corresponding to "boule" and "baguette" topologies. The experimental design compares the accuracy of Reasoning Traces (generating step-by-step proofs) against Direct Prediction (generating the final answer without intermediate steps) across three distinct large language model architectures. The methodology relies on synthetic data to rigorously control for topological variables, allowing for a clean analysis of how depth and breadth interact with reasoning mechanisms.

## Key Results
*   **Topology-Dependent Superiority:** RT models significantly outperform Direct Prediction (DP) on "boule" splits (shallow/broad), where generalization accuracy remains stable as breadth increases.
*   **Depth Penalty for Reasoning:** On "baguette" splits (deep/narrow), DP models surpass RT models by up to 50 percentage points, indicating that reasoning traces hinder generalization in deep tasks.
*   **Mechanism of Advantage:** Theoretical analysis via transitive inference tasks attributes the breadth advantage of RTs to an inductive bias that narrows the train-test gap, while the depth limitation stems from weak signal dispersion in long contexts.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The effectiveness of reasoning traces is not uniform but is governed by the interplay between inductive bias and statistical signal strength. In broad tasks, the repeated exposure to shallow logical structures reinforces specific inductive biases within the model, allowing reasoning traces to effectively narrow the gap between training and testing distributions. However, in deep tasks, the cumulative error and noise associated with generating long chains of reasoning degrade the signal. The mechanism shifts from benefiting from structured generalization to suffering from the statistical difficulty of maintaining coherent long-context dependencies.

## Limitations
*   **Synthetic Data Scope:** The reliance on propositional logic limits the external validity of findings to natural language or more complex semantic tasks.
*   **Training Regime Uncertainty:** The absence of zero-shot baseline comparisons introduces uncertainty regarding whether the DP advantage is purely topological or influenced by specific training configurations.
*   **Distribution Bias:** The specific distribution of the PITA dataset may artificially amplify the "weak signal" dispersion effect in deep contexts, potentially skewing the observed limits.

## Confidence
*   **Core Findings:** Medium. The experimental design rigorously isolates topology variables, but synthetic data limits ecological validity.
*   **RT vs. DP Dynamics:** High. Strong internal consistency across Qwen2.5-Coder, Gemma 3, and Llama 3 suggests a fundamental architectural bottleneck rather than model-specific overfitting.
