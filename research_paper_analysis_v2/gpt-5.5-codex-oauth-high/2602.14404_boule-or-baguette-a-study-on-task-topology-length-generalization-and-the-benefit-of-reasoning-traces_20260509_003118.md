# Boule or Baguette? A Study on Task Topology, Length Generalization, and the Benefit of Reasoning Traces

## Quick Facts

- **arXiv ID:** 2602.14404
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14404
- **Authors:** William L. Tong; Ege Cakar; Cengiz Pehlevan
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** Reasoning traces help length generalization on broad, shallow tasks, but can hurt on narrow, deep tasks where direct prediction often generalizes better.

## Executive Summary

This paper studies when reasoning traces improve or degrade length generalization in neural reasoning. It introduces **PITA**, a Lean-formalized propositional-logic dataset with over **23M statements** and **95B tokens**, where each statement is paired with proof traces and a final success/failure label.

The central finding is topology-dependent: reasoning-trace models perform better on **broad, shallow** task splits, while direct-prediction models outperform on **narrow, deep** splits. A synthetic transitive-inference task supports the same pattern and suggests that trace-based reasoning scales differently with breadth and depth than direct prediction.

## Method Summary

The paper evaluates length generalization for propositional-logic truth classification. Models train on statements whose proof lengths are at or below the split median, then test on longer-proof statements. PITA includes four task splits: **FULL**, **IMPLY**, **OR**, and **PHP**. Each example contains a Lean-formalized proposition and a proof trace ending in `<success />` or `<failure />`.

The main comparison is between **reasoning-trace (RT)** finetuning, where the completion includes proof steps plus the final tag, and **direct-prediction (DP)** finetuning, where the completion contains only the final classification tag. The authors finetune Qwen2.5-Coder, Gemma 3, and Llama 3 models with QLoRA using next-token cross-entropy on completion tokens only. Evaluation uses greedy decoding until `<success />` or `<failure />`; non-termination before the context limit is counted as an error.

## Key Results

- On broad, shallow splits such as **FULL** and **IMPLY**, RT models generally generalize better than DP baselines, especially for larger Qwen2.5-Coder models.
- On narrow, deep splits such as **OR** and **PHP**, DP models outperform RT models; on PHP, DP exceeds RT by up to roughly **50 percentage points** for a 7B model.
- In the synthetic transitive-inference task, DP generalization decays as **B⁻²** with breadth while RT stays flat; depth capacity scales as **D ∝ H** for DP but only **D ∝ √H** for RT, while breadth capacity scales as **B ∝ H²** for both.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### 1. Reasoning traces help when the task is broad but shallow

**Claim:** RTs improve length generalization when many surface forms share reusable proof structure.

**Mechanism:** In broad task spaces, many distinct statements can map to related proof patterns. Training on proof traces may bias models toward reusable intermediate transformations rather than brittle direct shortcuts. In the synthetic transitive-inference task, the RT format creates an enumeration-like representation where test cases can be permuted into train-like cases, supporting transfer when training accuracy is high.

**Core assumption:** PITA’s FULL and IMPLY splits share enough reusable proof structure that Lean proof traces expose a more transferable solution than final-label supervision alone.

**Evidence anchors:**
- The abstract reports that RT models generalize well on broad and shallow subsets.
- Section 2.3 reports RT outperforming DP on FULL and IMPLY.
- Section 3.3 attributes the synthetic TI effect to an RT inductive bias that transfers from train to test examples.

**Break condition:** This mechanism may fail if traces are noisy, non-canonical, too long, or if the broad task does not share reusable intermediate structure.

### 2. Reasoning traces hurt when deep tasks require long generation chains

**Claim:** RTs can degrade performance on narrow, deep tasks because long traces introduce extra failure modes.

**Mechanism:** Deep tasks require many proof steps. RT models must generate and process long contexts before producing the final classification token, creating opportunities for non-termination, cascading token errors, exposure-bias-like drift, and difficulty extracting weak signals from long contexts. The TI analysis supports this: RT depth capacity scales as **D ∝ √H**, slower than DP’s **D ∝ H**.

**Core assumption:** The PHP and OR degradation is partly caused by long proof-generation and context-processing burden, not only by insufficient model size or compute.

**Evidence anchors:**
- The abstract states that RT models deteriorate on narrow and deep subsets relative to non-RT baselines.
- Section 2.3 reports large DP-over-RT gaps on PHP.
- Figure 10 and Section 2.3 indicate that failure to terminate accounts for many RT errors.

**Break condition:** The effect may weaken with better long-context handling, trace compression, verifier-guided decoding, explicit termination control, or non-autoregressive proof-state planning.

### 3. Direct prediction can win when narrow tasks admit compact discriminative rules

**Claim:** DP can outperform RT on narrow, deep tasks when the task space is small enough for compact structure to generalize without proof emission.

**Mechanism:** DP avoids the cost of generating intermediate proof states. On narrow splits, fewer statement families may allow the model to learn a compact decision boundary or shortcut that remains valid beyond the training proof length. RT, by contrast, must execute every intermediate step and is penalized by long-context dynamics.

**Core assumption:** The compressed rule learned by DP on OR/PHP is aligned with test-time structure; this should not be assumed under broader or more shifted distributions.

**Evidence anchors:**
- The abstract states that DP models outperform on narrow, deep splits.
- Section 3.3 says DP models perform better on depth-dominated “baguette-shaped” tasks because depth capacity scales more favorably than in RT models.
- Section 4 argues for a nuanced view of reasoning traces even in propositional logic.

**Break condition:** DP’s advantage may disappear when breadth increases, shortcuts become spurious, examples require novel compositional structure, or labels cannot be inferred without intermediate state.

## Foundational Learning

### Propositional logic, tautology, and Lean proof states

- **Why needed:** PITA examples are formal propositions, not natural-language questions. Understanding `<success />`, `<failure />`, tactics, proof states, and backtracking is necessary to interpret the data.
- **Quick check:** Given `p → p ∨ q`, why is it true, and what Lean-style tactic might prove the disjunction?

### Task topology: depth versus breadth

- **Why needed:** The main result depends on distinguishing many unique statements from many required proof steps. FULL and IMPLY are broad-shallow; OR and PHP are narrow-deep.
- **Quick check:** If a dataset has few statement templates but proofs with hundreds of steps, is it more “boule” or “baguette”?

### Reasoning-trace versus direct-prediction training

- **Why needed:** RT models train on proof completions before the final classification token; DP models train only on the final tag.
- **Quick check:** Why can an RT model generate a syntactically plausible but wrong proof and still be evaluated only by its final classification token?

### Length generalization

- **Why needed:** The test set contains examples requiring longer proofs than those seen in training. The benchmark is not just in-distribution classification.
- **Quick check:** Why is high training accuracy insufficient evidence that a model can solve longer-proof examples?

### Non-termination as an evaluation failure mode

- **Why needed:** RT models may generate long traces without ever emitting `<success />` or `<failure />`, which is counted as incorrect.
- **Quick check:** How could a model that “keeps reasoning” perform worse than one that immediately predicts a label?

## Architecture Onboarding

### Component map

Dataset generator -> proof constructor -> Lean/XML formatter -> RT or DP training examples -> QLoRA finetuning stack -> greedy decoder -> final-tag evaluator

- **Dataset generator:** Enumerates or samples propositional statements for FULL, IMPLY, OR, and PHP.
- **Proof constructor:** Uses focused proof search to produce proof trees, including backtracking demonstrations.
- **Lean/XML formatter:** Converts tactics and proof states into tokenized prompt-completion examples.
- **Model variants:** RT completions include proof traces plus final tags; DP completions include only `<success />` or `<failure />`.
- **Training stack:** Uses pretrained Qwen, Gemma, and Llama models with QLoRA and next-token cross-entropy.
- **Evaluator:** Decodes until a success/failure tag or maximum context length; records accuracy and non-termination.

### Critical path

1. Reproduce one split end-to-end first, preferably OR because its membership-check structure is easy to inspect.
2. Verify prompt/completion boundaries and ensure loss is masked on prompt tokens.
3. Train DP and RT variants under identical train/test proof-length splits.
4. Confirm that evaluation counts non-termination as failure and does not require proof validity checking.

### Design tradeoffs

- RT gives richer supervision but increases sequence length and termination risk.
- DP is cheaper and shorter but more prone to shortcut learning on broad tasks.
- Canonical Lean traces reduce ambiguity but may not match the shortest or most human-like proof.
- Larger context windows help only if the model can use them reliably; PHP-like traces may still fail.

### Failure signatures

- RT emits long plausible XML but never reaches `<success />` or `<failure />`.
- RT chooses an invalid tactic and then continues with syntactically valid but semantically meaningless states.
- DP accuracy collapses on broad test examples despite high training accuracy.
- Class imbalance shift causes many false positives or false negatives across length splits.

### First 3 experiments

1. Run a small FULL/OR comparison with one 1B–3B model and check for the expected RT-over-DP pattern on FULL and DP-over-RT pattern on OR.
2. Add termination diagnostics: final-token accuracy, non-termination rate, average generated length, and false-positive/false-negative breakdown.
3. Test trace compression on PHP by comparing shorter or summarized proof states against full RT and DP.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### 1. Can task depth and breadth predict RT-vs-DP performance beyond propositional logic?

- **Basis in paper:** The authors explicitly call for characterizing topology more precisely and across a wider range of settings.
- **Why unresolved:** PITA and the synthetic TI task may not capture topology in natural language, math, planning, or code tasks.
- **What would resolve it:** Benchmarks with measured depth and breadth across multiple domains showing consistent RT-vs-DP performance boundaries.

### 2. What methods can make reasoning-trace models robust on deep tasks?

- **Basis in paper:** The authors identify long-context reasoning as an important obstacle and suggest alternatives to current Transformer paradigms are needed.
- **Why unresolved:** The paper observes failures but does not test architectural or training interventions.
- **What would resolve it:** Models with improved attention, memory, compression, or non-autoregressive trace mechanisms that close the PHP/deep-task gap.

### 3. How faithful and valid are the Lean proofs generated by RT models?

- **Basis in paper:** The authors defer a more thorough examination of LLM-generated proofs to future work.
- **Why unresolved:** Evaluation scores only the final classification token, not proof correctness.
- **What would resolve it:** Lean verification of generated traces, categorized tactic/state errors, and correlations between proof validity and final accuracy.

### 4. Do the TI scaling laws hold under harder negative examples?

- **Basis in paper:** The authors defer analysis of inverted negative examples to future work.
- **Why unresolved:** The main TI setup uses cross-branch negatives, which may simplify the learned decision rule.
- **What would resolve it:** Repeating breadth/depth/width scaling experiments with inverted negatives and comparing RT and DP exponents.

## Limitations

- Faithful reproduction is limited by unspecified dataset revision, exact train/test sample IDs, random seeds, and checkpoint/tokenizer versions.
- The proposed mechanism is partly inferential: the TI task supports the breadth/depth story, but does not prove the same mechanism drives all PITA results.
- Evaluation uses only the final `<success />` / `<failure />` tag, so proof validity and final-label correctness can diverge.

## Confidence

- **RT helps more on broad/shallow PITA splits while DP helps more on narrow/deep splits:** High
- **Long trace generation and non-termination materially contribute to RT degradation on deep tasks:** Medium
- **The synthetic TI mechanism fully explains the PITA results:** Low-to-Medium

## Next Checks

1. Reproduce one FULL split and one OR or PHP split with pinned dataset/model revisions and fixed seeds.
2. Measure accuracy, non-termination, generated length, and false-positive/false-negative rates separately.
3. Test compressed or verifier-guided RT formats to see whether reducing trace burden narrows the DP advantage on deep splits.
