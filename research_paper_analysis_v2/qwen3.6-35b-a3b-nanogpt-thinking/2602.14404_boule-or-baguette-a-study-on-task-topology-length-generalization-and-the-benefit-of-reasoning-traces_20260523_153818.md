# Boule or Baguette? A Study on Task Topology, Length Generalization, and the Benefit of Reasoning Traces

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2602.14404
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14404
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Reasoning traces significantly outperform direct prediction on broad, shallow logical tasks but degrade underperforming by up to 50 percentage points on narrow, deep tasks due to topology-dependent scaling limits.

## Executive Summary
This study investigates how task topology—specifically depth (reasoning steps) and breadth (unique example diversity)—dictates the efficacy of reasoning traces (RTs) versus direct prediction (DP) in neural networks. Introducing PITA, a 23 million statement propositional logic dataset, the authors reveal a consistent performance reversal: RTs excel on breadth-heavy tasks by imposing stable inductive biases, while they severely underperform DP on depth-heavy tasks due to long-context signal dilution.

Through synthetic transitive inference tasks and max-margin theoretical analysis, the paper establishes that RT generalization scales sublinearly with depth (D ∝ √H) and remains robust across breadth, whereas DP scales linearly with depth but suffers rapid generalization decay proportional to B⁻². These findings define fundamental, topology-dependent boundaries for reasoning architectures and highlight when step-by-step tracing is beneficial versus when it becomes a liability.

## Method Summary
The methodology evaluates length generalization across four PITA dataset splits (FULL, IMPLY, OR, PHP) and a parameterized synthetic Transitive Inference task. Models, including Qwen2.5-Coder, Gemma 3, and Llama 3 variants, are fine-tuned via QLoRA with dual configurations: RT models learn statement-plus-proof sequences, while DP models learn statement-plus-classification tokens. Evaluation uses greedy autoregressive decoding, masking prompts and computing accuracy strictly at termination tags on held-out splits requiring longer proofs than the training median.

Complementary theoretical baselines employ single-layer, single-head Transformers with μP initialization, analyzing max-margin optimization and total variation distance bounds to quantify feature mapping brittleness and long-context signal dilution. This combined empirical and analytical framework isolates how gradient descent navigates hypothesis spaces under varying depth and breadth constraints.

## Key Results
- RTs outperform DP on broad, shallow tasks, while DP outperforms RT on narrow, deep tasks by up to 50 percentage points.
- RT generalization scales sublinearly with depth (D ∝ √H) due to attention-based signal dilution, whereas DP scales linearly but decays quadratically with breadth (B⁻²).
- Reasoning traces enforce permutation-invariant inductive biases that stabilize long-range hypothesis mapping, while DP models collapse into brittle heuristic shortcuts under high example diversity.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Direct prediction (DP) models learn brittle heuristic shortcuts that degrade rapidly as task breadth expands.
- Mechanism: Gradient descent drives DP classifiers toward max-margin decision boundaries that overfit to training distribution patterns. When example diversity increases, these patterns lose statistical reliability, causing systematic false-negative predictions on unseen compositions. Assumption: Transformers prefer shallow feature mappings unless explicitly regularized by intermediate computation.
- Core assumption: Without explicit step-by-step grounding, models default to distributional proxies rather than structural rules.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "DP models discover suboptimal minima that generalize poorly for increasing task breadth." [section 3.3] Generalization accuracy decays proportional to B⁻² as the model overfits to training distribution shortcuts.
- Break condition: Test accuracy degrades according to an inverse-square relationship with breadth (αg ∝ B⁻²) when the hypothesis space exceeds the model’s capacity to maintain shortcut consistency.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Reasoning traces (RTs) impose a permutation-invariant inductive bias that stabilizes generalization on breadth-heavy tasks.
- Mechanism: By explicitly enumerating intermediate symbols or proof states, RT models effectively align test instances with training permutations. This alignment collapses the effective output variance, allowing the classifier to treat unseen long-range queries as reorganized training examples. Assumption: Intermediate state generation functions as an implicit normalization layer that reduces representational ambiguity.
- Core assumption: Explicit trace generation restricts the hypothesis space to structurally consistent mappings.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "RT generalization remains robust across increasing breadth but scales poorly with depth." [section B.4.1] Proposition B.4 demonstrates that any test example can be permuted into a training example, making generalization theoretically perfect under permutation invariance.
- Break condition: Generalization holds while breadth scales, but collapses once depth increases sufficiently to trigger long-context signal dilution, overriding the inductive bias advantage.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: RT models scale sublinearly with task depth (D ∝ √H) due to statistical indistinguishability in long sequences.
- Mechanism: As proof length grows, the attention mechanism distributes fixed representational capacity across more tokens, weakly dispersing the target signal. The total variation distance between positive and negative signal distributions shrinks asymptotically, rendering classification statistically infeasible without architectural compensation. Assumption: Transformer attention operates as a uniform averaging operator over longer contexts, limiting marginal information gain.
- Core assumption: Context window expansion degrades signal-to-noise ratio without compensatory capacity scaling.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "RTs... scale poorly with depth (D ∝ √H) due to long-context signal dilution." [section B.3.1] Proposition B.3 bounds total variation distance as TV(F, T) = O(√H/L), establishing a statistical ceiling on depth-wise scaling.
- Break condition: Model accuracy plateaus or degrades when task depth exceeds O(√H), as distributional distinguishability falls below the binary classification threshold.

## Architecture Onboarding
- Component map: Topology splitter -> Dataset pipeline (PITA/TL proof generator) -> Model trainer (RT/DP dual-config) -> Evaluation harness (length generalization counter, termination tracker) -> Theoretical analyzer (margin estimator, TV distance calculator)
- Critical path: Split data by topology -> Finetune RT and DP baselines -> Run length generalization eval -> Log accuracy, false negative rate, and termination failures -> Plot scaling curves (B vs. H, D vs. H)
- Design tradeoffs: RT training incurs higher compute and context overhead but suppresses shortcut overfitting; DP trains faster but requires strict distribution control to avoid brittle generalization. Context window size directly caps depth scalability; model width scales breadth quadratically but depth linearly (DP) or square-root (RT).
- Failure signatures: RT: high non-termination rate, flat accuracy across breadth sweeps, rapid degradation past D > √H. DP: systematic false negatives, accuracy decaying as B⁻², extreme sensitivity to variable naming or syntactic permutations.
- First 3 experiments:
  1. Replicate boule/baguette performance reversal on FULL vs PHP splits using a fixed-width 7B model to confirm the generalization gap.
  2. Sweep breadth B at fixed depth D for both RT and DP baselines while logging false negative rates to isolate shortcutting decay.
  3. Sweep depth D at fixed breadth to map the RT context dilution curve, measuring generation length and termination failure rate as proxies for TV distance convergence.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
1. Do the topology-dependent RT vs. DP reversal trends generalize beyond formal logic to natural language and complex mathematical reasoning? The authors state it remains unclear whether these results are idiosyncratic to PITA or indicative of broader phenomena. Resolving this requires controlled evaluations across diverse domains (e.g., program synthesis, NLP deduction) while systematically varying task depth and breadth to track performance crossover points.
2. Can architectural or input-format interventions mitigate the long-context signal dilution that causes RT underperformance on deep tasks? The paper identifies signal dilution as the fundamental bottleneck for RT depth scaling. Empirical tests of alternative trace formats (e.g., truncated enumerations, retrieval-augmented steps) would determine whether the depth-scaling barrier can approach the linear D ∝ H regime of DP models.
3. How do adaptive reasoning strategies and omitted test structures (e.g., inverted negatives) affect the breadth-depth generalization tradeoff? The theoretical model assumes fixed, full symbol enumeration, ignoring search-based, pruned, or self-generated reasoning paths. Comparative training on pruned, backward-chain, or model-generated traces is needed to quantify their impact on depth generalization and error distribution.
4. How do positional encoding schemes interact with the breadth-scaling advantages of reasoning traces? The TI margin analysis explicitly omits positional encodings for tractability, despite their documented necessity for Transformer length generalization. Ablation studies across positional schemes (RoPE, ALiBi, learned) are required to determine how they modulate the B ∝ H² breadth scaling regime.

## Limitations
- Findings are constrained to synthetic propositional logic and simplified theoretical Transformers, limiting direct transfer to natural language or complex mathematical benchmarks.
- Full replication is blocked by unreleased proof-search hyperparameters, exact Lean tactic filters, and synthetic TI convergence thresholds.
- Theoretical depth-bounds rely on restricted single-layer, uniform-attention models that diverge from standard multilayer, multi-head LLM architectures.

## Confidence
- Topology-dependent RT/DP performance reversal: High
- DP shortcut collapse scaling (accuracy decays as B⁻²): Medium
- RT depth degradation scaling (D ∝ √H via signal dilution): Medium
