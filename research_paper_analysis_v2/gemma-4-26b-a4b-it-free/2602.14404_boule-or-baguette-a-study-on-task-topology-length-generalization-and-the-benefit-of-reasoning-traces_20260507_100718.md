# Boule or Baguette? A Study on Task Topology, Length Generalization, and the Benefit of Reasoning Traces

## Quick Facts
* **arXiv ID:** 2602.14404
* **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14404
* **Reference Count:** 40
* **Primary Result:** Reasoning trace (RT) models excel on broad/shallow tasks ("boule") but fail to generalize to deep/narrow tasks ("baguette") where direct prediction (DP) models perform better.

## Executive Summary
This study investigates the performance limits of reasoning trace (RT) models—neural networks that generate intermediate steps before a final answer—specifically regarding their ability to generalize to longer tasks than seen during training. By categorizing tasks via "task topology," the researchers identify a fundamental trade-off in how LLMs handle reasoning depth versus breadth.

The research demonstrates that while RT models provide significant benefits for "boule-shaped" tasks (high breadth, low depth), they suffer significant performance degradation on "baguette-shaped" tasks (low breadth, high depth). In deep reasoning scenarios, such as the Pigeonhole Principle, direct prediction models can outperform RT models by as much as 50 percentage points.

## Method Summary
The authors introduce PITA, a large-scale dataset comprising over 23 million propositional logic statements paired with Lean-formalized proofs. This dataset allows for rigorous testing of reasoning capabilities in a controlled, formal environment.

Tasks are categorized using a "task topology" framework defined by two dimensions: breadth (the number of unique examples) and depth (the number of required reasoning steps). The researchers compared RT models against Direct Prediction (DP) baselines across multiple major model families, including Qwen, Gemma, and Llama, to observe how performance scales with task complexity.

## Key Results
* **The Topology Trade-off:** RT models significantly outperform DP models on "boule-shaped" tasks (broad/shallow) as breadth increases.
* **Depth-Driven Failure:** On "baguette-shaped" tasks (narrow/deep), RT models deteriorate relative to DP baselines.
* **Extreme Performance Gap:** On the Pigeonhole Principle (PHP) split, DP models outperformed RT models by up to 50 percentage points.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
Theoretical analysis suggests that the failure of RT models on deep tasks is driven by the statistical difficulty of processing long contexts. In these "baguette" scenarios, the essential reasoning signal becomes weakly dispersed across the extended sequence, making it harder for the model to maintain coherence and logical progression.

## Foundational Learning
* **Task Topology:** Needed to quantify the geometric structure of reasoning problems. *Quick check: Is the task defined by many unique examples or many sequential steps?*
* **Reasoning Traces (RT):** Needed to model intermediate cognitive steps. *Quick check: Does the model output a chain of thought before the answer?*
* **Direct Prediction (DP):** Needed as a baseline for end-to-end mapping. *Quick check: Does the model map input directly to the final output?*
* **Propositional Logic:** Needed to provide a ground-truth formal environment. *Quick check: Are the statements verifiable via formal proof engines like Lean?*
* **Signal Dispersion:** Needed to explain context-length performance decay. *Quick check: Does the relevance of the signal decrease as the sequence length grows?*

## Architecture Onboarding
**Component Map**
Input Proposition -> Reasoning Trace (Intermediate Steps) -> Final Answer

**Critical Path**
Input -> Token Generation (Trace) -> Logical Consistency Check -> Final Output Prediction

**Design Tradeoffs**
* **RT Approach:** Higher accuracy on diverse, shallow tasks; higher risk of signal dispersion in deep tasks.
* **DP Approach:** More robust in deep, narrow tasks; lacks the "thinking" capacity for complex multi-step breadth.

**Failure Signatures**
* **Baguette Failure:** Rapid accuracy decay as the number of reasoning steps (depth) increases beyond training limits.
* **Signal Weakness:** High variance in output quality as the context window expands during long proofs.

## Limitations
* **Domain Specificity:** Results are derived from propositional logic; it is unclear if "baguette" degradation occurs in semantic-heavy natural language reasoning.
* **Model Scope:** The study focuses on standard LLM families, leaving the behavior of specialized reasoning models (e.g., OpenAI o1) unknown.

## Confidence
* **Task Topology Trade-off (Boule vs. Baguette):** High
* **Signal Dispersion Theory:** Medium
* **Generalizability to Natural Language:** Low

## Next Checks
1. **Cross-Domain Stress Test:** Evaluate the "baguette" degradation on the GSM8K or MATH datasets to see if depth-driven failure is domain-specific to formal logic.
2. **Architectural Sensitivity Analysis:** Test if models with expanded context windows or different attention mechanisms (e.g., Ring Attention) mitigate the signal dispersion in deep tasks.
3. **Trace Density Modulation:** Experiment with varying the granularity of the reasoning traces (shorter vs. more verbose steps) to determine if there is an optimal "trace density" that stabilizes deep task performance.
