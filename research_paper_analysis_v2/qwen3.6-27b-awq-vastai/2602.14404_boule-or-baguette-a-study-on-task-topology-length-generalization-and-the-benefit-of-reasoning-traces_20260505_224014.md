# Boule or Baguette? A Study on Task Topology, Length Generalization, and the Benefit of Reasoning Traces

## Quick Facts
* **arXiv ID:** 2602.14404
* **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14404
* **Authors:** William L. Tong; Ege Cakar; Cengiz Pehlevan
* **Reference count:** 40
* **Primary Result:** Reasoning traces exhibit a topology-dependent performance reversal, excelling on broad, shallow tasks via permutation invariance but degrading on narrow, deep tasks due to signal dilution, whereas direct prediction scales linearly with depth but collapses on breadth due to shortcut overfitting.

## Executive Summary
The study establishes fundamental limits on when reasoning traces (RTs) benefit neural network reasoning by analyzing length generalization across task topologies defined by depth and breadth. Using PITA, a dataset of 23 million propositional logic statements, the authors demonstrate that RTs significantly outperform direct prediction (DP) on broad, shallow tasks by enforcing permutation-invariant inductive biases. Conversely, RTs degrade on narrow, deep tasks, underperforming DP by up to 50 percentage points due to long-context signal dilution.

Theoretical analysis and synthetic benchmarks reveal that RT depth generalization scales as $D \propto \sqrt{H}$ while DP scales linearly with depth ($D \propto H$), though DP suffers breadth generalization decay proportional to $B^{-2}$ from reliance on brittle local heuristics. These findings provide a topology-aware framework for selecting reasoning paradigms, showing that the utility of reasoning traces is conditional on task structure rather than universally beneficial.

## Method Summary
Evaluation focuses on length generalization using the PITA dataset of Lean-formalized propositional statements and a synthetic transitive inference benchmark. Models are fine-tuned via QLoRA on decoder-only LLMs, comparing RT inputs (XML-templated proofs with full tactic/state enumeration) against DP inputs (immediate classification). Training applies next-token prediction with loss masking to completion tokens, optimizing over 2,500 steps with a context window of 32k. Generalization accuracy is measured on test examples exceeding the training median proof length or relation distance, with scaling analysis performed across contours of breadth, depth, and context length to isolate generalization boundaries.

## Key Results
* RTs significantly outperform DP on broad, shallow tasks but reverse to underperform DP on narrow, deep tasks, with accuracy drops up to 50 percentage points on the deepest splits.
* RT generalization scales poorly with depth ($D \propto \sqrt{H}$) due to long-context signal dilution, while DP scales linearly with depth ($D \propto H$) but suffers generalization decay proportional to $B^{-2}$ on broad tasks.
* Permutation-invariant attention in RT models enables robust breadth generalization by allowing test examples to be reordered into structurally equivalent training instances, whereas DP models accumulate spurious correlations that drive systematic false negatives.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1: Permutation-Invariant Inductive Bias in RTs
* **Claim:** Reasoning traces conditionally improve generalization on broad, shallow tasks by enforcing permutation-invariant inductive biases.
* **Mechanism:** The RT input format enumerates all symbols in a reasoning chain. Without positional encodings, attention weights converge to uniform averaging, rendering the model invariant to permutations of intermediate tokens. This allows any test example to be reordered into a structurally equivalent training example, preserving accuracy as task space expands.
* **Core assumption:** The underlying logical dependencies are permutation-invariant relative to the query token, and the model operates in a rich learning regime where uniform attention emerges.
* **Evidence anchors:** Abstract ("RTs significantly outperformed DP on broad, shallow tasks"); Proposition B.4 & Section B.5.1 (permutation mapping guarantees perfect test accuracy when training accuracy is saturated).
* **Break condition:** Fails when task structure depends on strict sequential ordering, or when learned positional encodings re-introduce sequence-dependent biases that break permutation equivalence.

### Mechanism 2: Long-Context Signal Dilution in RTs
* **Claim:** Reasoning traces degrade on narrow, deep tasks due to long-context signal dilution.
* **Mechanism:** As reasoning depth increases, the RT lengthens proportionally. The discriminative signal (e.g., whether the query symbol belongs to the enumerated branch) becomes statistically dispersed across many tokens. The total variation distance between positive and negative example distributions shrinks asymptotically, limiting depth scaling to $D \propto \sqrt{H}$ rather than linear.
* **Core assumption:** Symbol embeddings are isotropic and attention weights average uniformly across the context window, making signal extraction a statistical distinguishability problem.
* **Evidence anchors:** Abstract ("scales poorly with depth ($D \propto \sqrt{H}$) due to long-context signal dilution"); Section B.3.1 & Proposition B.3 (TV distance bound $O(\sqrt{H}/L)$ derived from KL divergence analysis).
* **Break condition:** Fails when architectural modifications (e.g., sparse/retentive attention, hierarchical compression, or explicit signal routing) preserve discriminative feature concentration over extended contexts.

### Mechanism 3: Shortcut Overfitting in Direct Prediction
* **Claim:** Direct prediction models suffer generalization decay on broad tasks due to reliance on brittle, local shortcut heuristics.
* **Mechanism:** DP models maximize training margin by learning local coordinate patterns that exploit distributional regularities. As breadth increases, spurious negative correlations accumulate across branches, driving output logits negative and producing systematic false negatives. RTs bypass this by forcing explicit stepwise decomposition that captures global task structure.
* **Core assumption:** Gradient descent in overparameterized networks converges to max-margin solutions that preferentially fit local heuristics when global compositional rules are harder to isolate.
* **Evidence anchors:** Abstract ("DP ... suffers generalization decay proportional to $B^{-2}$"); Section B.4.1 & B.5 (logit decomposition $\hat{y} = \hat{y}_0 - \beta$ and false negative rate scaling analysis).
* **Break condition:** Fails when training data is heavily constrained, or when regularization/distillation forces DP models to learn global compositional features rather than local correlations.

## Foundational Learning

### Concept: Length Generalization
* **Why needed here:** Serves as the primary metric for evaluating whether models truly learn algorithmic reasoning versus memorizing sequence lengths.
* **Quick check question:** If a model is trained on proofs up to depth 5, what accuracy do you expect at depth 10, and what architectural factor determines the gap?

### Concept: Task Topology (Depth vs. Breadth)
* **Why needed here:** Provides a formal axis to predict whether RT or DP will be optimal, separating step-count constraints from task-space diversity.
* **Quick check question:** Does a split with 10,000 unique propositions and 3 reasoning steps favor RT or DP, and why?

### Concept: Max-Margin Implicit Bias
* **Why needed here:** Explains why overparameterized networks converge to specific weight structures that dictate generalization boundaries under different input formats.
* **Quick check question:** Why does a wide network trained to zero training loss still exhibit different test generalization when given proofs versus direct labels?

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map:** PITA dataset (Lean-formalized statements + focused proof search) -> RT input pipeline (XML-templated prompt + full tactic/state enumeration + termination token) -> DP input pipeline (prompt + immediate classification token) -> TI synthetic task (B parallel chains × D depth symbols) -> Theoretical analysis module (sample-coordinate weight decomposition, margin constraints, TV distance bounds)

**Critical path:** Data generation -> XML templating & tokenization -> QLoRA fine-tuning -> Length generalization evaluation -> Scaling analysis (B, D, H contours) -> Error breakdown (TP/FP/TN/FN/non-termination)

**Design tradeoffs:**
* Canonical proofs guarantee verifiable RTs but inflate context length.
* DP is computationally efficient but vulnerable to shortcut overfitting.
* Omitting positional encodings enables permutation invariance but breaks tasks requiring strict ordering.
* Lean formalization removes linguistic noise but limits direct NLP transfer.

**Failure signatures:**
* RT non-termination (max context hit).
* Systematic false negatives on high-breadth DP splits.
* Accuracy plateaus before median proof length.
* OOM errors on PHP/long-context splits.
* Uniform attention collapse in TI baselines.

**First 3 experiments:**
1. Replicate PITA IMPLY vs OR splits with Qwen2.5-Coder-7B to verify the topology-dependent performance reversal.
2. Replace full XML proof enumeration with a truncated/summarized RT format to quantify signal dilution sensitivity under fixed depth.
3. Inject learned sinusoidal positional encodings into the TI transformer to break permutation invariance and measure the resulting breadth generalization drop.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
* **Question:** How can long-context processing be improved in RT models to maintain accuracy on deep, narrow tasks?
* **Basis in paper:** Explicit discussion notes it is "more feasible to improve long-context processing in an RT model" for challenging tasks.
* **Why unresolved:** Current Transformers suffer from signal dilution and statistical indistinguishability over long autoregressive generations ($D \propto \sqrt{H}$ scaling limit).
* **What evidence would resolve it:** Benchmarks testing novel attention mechanisms or positional encodings on deep, narrow logical/mathematical datasets.

### Open Question 2
* **Question:** Can task depth and breadth metrics be precisely characterized across diverse domains to predict when direct prediction outperforms RTs?
* **Basis in paper:** Explicit anticipation of "future work characterizing topology more precisely and across a wider range of settings."
* **Why unresolved:** Metrics are currently validated only on propositional logic and transitive inference; transferability to code, math, or planning is untested.
* **What evidence would resolve it:** Systematic RT vs DP evaluations on cross-domain benchmarks with measured depth/breadth analogs correlated to generalization gaps.

### Open Question 3
* **Question:** How do inverted negative examples in transitive inference affect RT versus DP generalization tradeoffs?
* **Basis in paper:** Section 3.1 explicitly defers analysis of "i > j inverted negative examples for future work."
* **Why unresolved:** Theoretical and empirical analysis omits intra-branch reversed relations, focusing only on cross-branch negatives.
* **What evidence would resolve it:** Re-running TI experiments with inverted negatives included, tracking shifts in max-margin weight structures and length generalization decay.

### Open Question 4
* **Question:** What systematic methods can evaluate the logical validity of LLM-generated reasoning traces beyond final accuracy?
* **Basis in paper:** Footnote 3 defers "a more thorough examination of LLM-generated proofs for future work."
* **Why unresolved:** Evaluation only measures the final classification token, masking intermediate hallucinations or unfaithful step-wise reasoning.
* **What evidence would resolve it:** Application of automated proof verifiers or step-level consistency metrics that correlate trace validity with terminal accuracy.

## Limitations
* Theoretical bounds assume uniform attention and isotropic symbol embeddings, which may not hold in modern sparse or hybrid architectures.
* The PITA proof generation pipeline depends on external Lean toolchains with undocumented backtrack heuristics, risking hidden bias in trace structure.
* Length generalization isolates sequence extension but does not decouple logical branching complexity from depth, and XML tokenizer boundaries vary across model families, introducing uncontrolled prompt variance.

## Confidence
* **Topology-dependent RT/DP reversal:** High
* **Permutation-invariance mechanism:** Medium (Relies on unverified attention collapse under RT formatting)
* **Max-margin shortcut bias in DP:** Medium (Aligns with prior work but not explicitly isolated)
* **Signal dilution scaling ($D \propto \sqrt{H}$):** Medium (Mathematically sound but highly sensitive to context window and attention implementation)
